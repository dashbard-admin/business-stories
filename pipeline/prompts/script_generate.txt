here should be unable to put the video down at 30 seconds, 5 minutes,

FACT LEDGER — these are the ONLY facts you may state as true:
{fact_ledger_json}

If you need to state something not in the fact ledger, you must
either (a) phrase it as explicit speculation ("biographers have
suggested..."), or (b) omit it. NEVER invent dates, dollar amounts,
quotes, names, founder dialogue, valuations, employee counts, or
court findings not present in the ledger.

STRUCTURE — three structural laws + one wildcard.

LAW 1 — COLD OPEN (mandatory, first 60-100 words, BEFORE the
"## BEAT 1 ##" marker):
Open with the central tension of THIS story, named in plain language.
Within the first three sentences the viewer must hear:
- the company name,
- the year anchor (or the era),
- and ONE specific concrete detail that signals this is not the story
  they think it is — a number that doesn't fit the narrative, a
  decision that contradicted everything else the founder said, a
  competitor who turned out to be right, a filing nobody read.

Do NOT open with weather, abstract context, or a CEO biography. The
viewer decides whether to keep watching in the first 8 seconds; the
cold open is the only paragraph that wins or loses that decision.

The cold open must NAME the hero and the conflict somewhere in its
first three sentences. The rest of the script earns them.

LAW 2 — ARCHETYPE BACKBONE (the rest of the script):
Follow the archetype guidance for opening device, middle structure,
and closing device. The cold open runs THROUGH the archetype — the
archetype shapes how the question gets developed from paragraph two
onward. Do not start the archetype opening from scratch after the
cold open; weave it in.

LAW 3 — THREE FORWARD-TEASE MOMENTS at roughly 25%, 50%, and 75% of
the script:
Each tease is exactly ONE sentence that names something the script
will return to later. The substance MUST come from the fact ledger —
a filing whose full implications will be unpacked later, a competitor
not yet named, a financial metric that doesn't match the press
release, a decision the founder later regretted.

Acceptable patterns:
  - "The Series A had a clause that wouldn't matter for another four
    years — we will come back to it after the pivot."
  - "What the company's own 2003 annual report revealed about that
    quarter would take outside analysts a decade to notice."
  - "The competitor that would eventually buy them was, in 1999, a
    customer."

Banned tease patterns: "you won't believe", "the shocking truth",
any second-person command ("wait until you hear what they did
next"), any tease promising something not in the fact ledger.

WILDCARD — keep ONE deliberate unpredictable element, placed
organically (NOT at the same beat position as previous episodes):
- An unexpected zoom-out to the broader market context
- A direct quotation from the founder, read verbatim
- A direct address to the viewer (one time only)
- An admission of what is NOT documented
- A specific number that recontextualizes everything ("$8.4 million in
  the bank, with thirty-one employees — and the rent was due Friday")

EDITORIAL CLOSING — mandatory, 200-300 words.
The final 200-300 words must be the narrator's perspective on what
THIS specific story reveals about founders, markets, or the era.
Make it specific to THIS company — not a generic closing. It must
LAND: end on a single concrete image or sentence the viewer will
remember. Do NOT summarize the episode. Do NOT moralize. Pay off the
cold-open question.

DO NOT use any of these phrasings or moves:
- Sensational opening hooks ("What you are about to hear will shock you")
- Clickbait phrasings ("They never expected what happened next")
- AI disclaimers ("As an AI...")
- The words "shocking", "unbelievable", "horrifying", "you won't believe",
  "game-changer", "disrupt the industry", "rocked the world",
  "perfect storm", "rocketed", "skyrocketed"
- "Tragic" more than twice in the whole script
- Rounding revenue / valuation up for drama
- Speculation about founder's inner monologue not in the ledger
- Made-up dialogue not present in the fact ledger
- Summary-style closing ("In conclusion...", "And so we have seen...")
- MBA-textbook jargon ("synergies", "value proposition",
  "stakeholder alignment") unless the source itself uses it

DO:
- Use specific dates (Month Day, Year) when in the ledger
- Use exact dollar amounts (rounded only to the nearest precision the
  source itself used)
- Cite filings, courts, or reports by name when quoting
- Acknowledge uncertainty where the ledger flags conflict
- Vary sentence length aggressively — short jabs interleaved with
  occasional longer densified paragraphs
- Plant a new specific question, name, or filing reference every
  2-3 minutes (every ~8 beats) so the viewer always has something
  to resolve
- Pay off planted questions with concrete answers
- Include 3-5 short paragraphs (under 30 words) for pacing breath
- Include 2-3 longer paragraphs (80-120 words) for narrative density
- Treat the HERO as a person, not a logo. Use first name on second
  reference; later, surname only.
